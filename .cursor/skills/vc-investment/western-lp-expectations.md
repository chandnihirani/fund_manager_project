# What Western LPs Look For: Emerging Market Funds

**Context:** DFIs, foundations, family offices, and HNWIs (including those who invest directly, not via a family office) investing in funds that deploy in non-Western markets (Africa, LatAm, Southeast Asia, etc.). This document captures what each investor type prioritises so the skill can assess managers against the right expectations.

**Impact is fund- and vehicle-specific, not org-type-specific.** A DFI may have a commercial-only vehicle; a family office may have an Africa-focused pot that is impact-driven while the rest of the book is commercial. The skill must not assume “DFI = impact” or “family office = commercial.” Only give impact-related feedback after looking up what the **specific fund or vehicle named by the user** says they focus on (see “Impact assessment: named fund only” below).

---

## Overview

| Investor type | Primary lens | Track record weight | Other heavy priorities |
|---------------|----------------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| **DFIs** | Development impact + financial sustainability (varies by fund/vehicle) | Strong; often minimum bar (years, geography, role) | Governance, ESG, terms, reporting, country alignment |
| **Foundations** | Mission alignment + impact (varies by program) | Important; sometimes willing to back first-time if thesis/team strong | Impact measurement, theory of change, who else is in |
| **Family offices** | Risk/return + relationship; may have impact-focused sleeves | Very high; referenceability and proof matter a lot | Governance, transparency, co-invest optionality |
| **HNWIs (direct)** | Highly variable; relationship + thesis; may be impact or commercial | High; proof and trust matter; often no formal office to diligence | Relationship, clarity, referenceability, alignment with personal goals |

**Impact:** Whether an investor cares about impact track record depends on the **specific fund or program** they are investing through, not only on whether they are a DFI, foundation, or family office. Always verify by looking up the named fund’s stated focus before giving impact feedback.

---

## Impact assessment: named fund only

**Do not assume impact focus from investor type.** Organisations run multiple vehicles with different mandates (e.g. commercial fund vs. impact-focused Africa pot). The skill must:

1. **Only give feedback on impact track record when the user has named specific LP(s) or fund(s).** If no named target is given, do not raise impact as a criterion or gap.
2. **Before giving any impact-related feedback, search the web** for what that **named fund or vehicle** (e.g. “BII’s African investment team,” “FMO’s SME fund,” “XYZ Family Office’s emerging markets sleeve”) publicly states they focus on — impact themes, financial-first, dual mandate, etc.
3. **Tailor impact feedback only to what the named fund states.** If the fund’s website or press says they prioritise development impact, jobs, gender, climate, etc., then assess the manager’s impact track record and narrative against that. If the named fund is described as commercial or return-focused, do not frame missing impact evidence as a gap; focus on financial track record and governance.
4. **Cite the source.** When giving impact feedback, reference what the fund says (e.g. “Fund X states they focus on Y; your materials show Z — alignment/gap here”).

This applies to all investor types: DFIs, foundations, family offices, and HNWIs. Impact expectations are set by the **vehicle**, not the org label.

## DFIs (Development Finance Institutions)

**Examples:** IFC, CDC Group, FMO, Proparco, DEG, Finnfund, Norfund, BIO, etc.

### What they are optimising for

- **Dual mandate:** Development impact (jobs, gender, climate, SDGs, additionality) and financial sustainability. Not grant; they expect risk-adjusted returns and capital back.
- **Additionality:** They want to invest where their capital makes a difference (e.g. first-time funds, underserved geographies, sectors that need patient capital). Emerging managers in non-Western markets often fit.
- **Risk discipline:** Political, currency, execution, governance. They have internal risk frameworks and need managers who understand and manage these.

### What they look for in a manager / fund

| Area | What DFIs look for |
|------|---------------------|
| **Track record** | Evidence of investing in the **same or adjacent geography and sector**. Clear attribution (who led, check sizes). Realized outcomes help; paper marks are secondary. First-time funds: pre-fund experience (angel, syndicate, prior fund role) must be clearly attributable and relevant. |
| **Governance** | Strong governance: independent advisory committee or board, clear decision rights, conflict policies. Fund structure and GP/LP terms that meet institutional norms. |
| **ESG / E&S** | Environmental and social risk management (often IFC Performance Standards or equivalent). Capacity to assess and monitor E&S in portfolio companies; no tolerance for obvious E&S failures. |
| **Impact** | How the fund contributes to development outcomes (jobs, gender, climate, financial inclusion, etc.). Metrics, baseline, and reporting commitment. Aligned with DFI’s own impact framework where possible. |
| **Terms** | Fees, carry, key person, investment period, reserve policy. DFIs often have specific requirements (e.g. lower fees, governance rights, reporting). Misalignment here can kill a process. |
| **Reporting** | High-quality financial and impact reporting. Annual audited accounts, capital call/distribution mechanics, transparency on portfolio and valuations. |
| **Country / strategy fit** | Alignment with the DFI’s country or regional strategy (where they are allowed to deploy). Some DFIs have restricted geographies or sectors. |

### Implications for the skill

- Track record must show **geography and sector relevance** and **attribution**; DFIs will probe both.
- Governance and ESG are table stakes; a manager who has no answer on E&S or governance will not pass.
- Impact is not optional; narrative and metrics must be clear even at first meeting.
- Terms and reporting expectations should be understood early; mismatches are a common blind spot.

## Foundations (Mission-Related / Program-Related Investment)

**Examples:** Gates Foundation, Rockefeller, MacArthur, Ford, Omidyar, Mulago, Co-Impact, etc.

### What they are optimising for

- **Mission alignment:** Health, education, financial inclusion, climate, gender, livelihoods — the fund’s thesis must align with the foundation’s priorities.
- **Impact:** Often catalytic or concessionary capital; some accept below-market returns for high impact. They still care about governance and credibility.
- **Theory of change:** How does deploying capital in this fund achieve the outcomes they care about? Clarity on causality (capital → companies → impact).

### What they look for in a manager / fund

| Area | What foundations look for |
|------|----------------------------|
| **Track record** | Relevant experience in the **sector and geography** they care about. First-time managers can be acceptable if thesis is sharp and team has demonstrable edge (operating experience, network, prior investing in the space). Attribution and relevance matter; vague “we’ve been in the ecosystem” is weak. |
| **Impact measurement** | How will the fund measure impact? Metrics, baseline, reporting. Alignment with foundation’s own framework (e.g. IRIS+, GIIN, custom). Willingness to report on outcomes, not just deployment. |
| **Mission fit** | Clear link between fund strategy and foundation mission. Sector and geography must match; “we do a bit of everything” is unpersuasive. |
| **Capital structure** | Who else is in? Other foundations, DFIs, commercial LPs. First capital in can be catalytic; they often want to see that others will follow or that terms are “market” enough for others to join. |
| **Governance** | Solid governance and transparency; less formal than DFIs in some cases, but serious LPs expect clear structure and reporting. |
| **Relationship** | Foundations often invest in people and thesis; relationship and trust matter. They may do more diligence on the team’s commitment and integrity than on pure financial metrics. |

### Implications for the skill

- Track record should emphasise **sector and geography relevance** and **edge** (why this team can deliver impact and returns in this space).
- Impact narrative and measurement are central; weak or generic impact framing is a gap.
- First-time is not a disqualifier if thesis and team are strong; the bar is “can we trust and verify?”

## Family Offices (Investing in Emerging Market Funds)

**Examples:** Single-family and multi-family offices that allocate to VC/PE in emerging markets. Highly heterogeneous.

### What they are optimising for

- **Risk/return:** Clarity on expected return, risk (currency, political, liquidity), and how the manager mitigates. Many are cautious in emerging markets and need to be comfortable with the story.
- **Governance and transparency:** Clear terms, reporting, and access to information. They often have less internal capacity than DFIs/foundations, so they rely on manager quality and transparency.
- **Relationship and access:** Personal relationship with GP; sometimes co-investment or deal access. They want to understand who they’re backing.
- **Referenceability:** Who else has invested? Can they talk to other LPs or co-investors? Track record that can be verified matters a lot.

### What they look for in a manager / fund

| Area | What family offices look for |
|------|------------------------------|
| **Track record** | **Proof and verifiability** are critical. Clear attribution, realized outcomes, referenceable deals and co-investors. Vague or unverifiable track records are a red flag. Solo GPs need to show even more clearly what they did. |
| **Terms** | Fees, carry, key person, liquidity. Alignment with their expectations; no surprises. They may be less standardised than DFIs but still want clarity. |
| **Reporting** | Regular, clear reporting (portfolio, valuations, capital account). They may not have large teams to parse complex reports. |
| **Risk narrative** | How does the manager think about currency, political, and execution risk? Demonstrated awareness and discipline. |
| **Co-investment** | Some family offices want optionality to co-invest; mentioning capacity and process can help. |
| **Who else is in** | Other respected LPs (DFIs, foundations, institutional) can be a signal; first or only LP is harder. |

### Implications for the skill

- Track record must be **verifiable and attributable**; family offices have fewer ways to cross-check in emerging markets, so clarity and references matter more.
- Governance and transparency are table stakes; weak reporting or opaque structure is a gap.
- Risk awareness (currency, political, execution) should be explicit in how the manager presents themselves.
- **Impact:** Do not assume the family office is commercial. They may have impact-focused sleeves or region-specific pots. Only comment on impact after looking up what the **named** fund or vehicle states.

## HNWIs (High-Net-Worth Individuals) — direct investors

**Context:** Individuals who invest in emerging-market funds in their own name or through a simple structure, **not** via a dedicated family office. They may be angels, syndicate leads, or LP allocators.

### What they are optimising for

- **Relationship and trust:** Direct connection with the GP; they want to know who they are backing and often invest in people as much as in the thesis.
- **Clarity on thesis and use of capital:** Simple, clear story: geography, sector, stage, and how their capital fits. Less formal process than institutions but expectations still exist.
- **Risk/return or impact (or both):** Highly variable. Some are purely return-focused; others have a strong impact or regional (e.g. Africa) motivation. The **specific person or vehicle** sets the bar — do not assume.
- **Referenceability and proof:** Often no internal team to do deep diligence; they rely on who else is in, referenceable deals, and verifiable track record. Vague or unverifiable claims are a red flag.

### What they look for in a manager / fund

| Area | What HNWIs (direct) look for |
|------|------------------------------|
| **Track record** | **Attribution and proof** matter. Clear role, check sizes, realized outcomes where possible. Referenceable co-investors or LPs help. Solo GPs need to be especially clear. |
| **Thesis** | Clear, focused strategy (geography, sector, stage). “We do a bit of everything” is unpersuasive. |
| **Terms** | Fees, carry, key person — clarity and alignment with their expectations. Less standardised than DFIs but surprises are poorly received. |
| **Reporting** | Regular, understandable updates (portfolio, valuations, capital account). They may not have staff to parse complex reports. |
| **Relationship** | Access to the GP, transparency, and sense that their capital is taken seriously. Co-invest or deal flow optionality can matter. |
| **Impact (if relevant)** | Only if that **specific** investor or their stated vehicle is impact-focused. Do not assume; look up or ask. |

### Implications for the skill

- Track record must be **attributable and verifiable**; HNWIs often have fewer ways to cross-check, so clarity and references matter.
- Do not assume commercial vs. impact; the individual or their named vehicle may be impact-driven (e.g. “Africa impact” pot). Only give impact feedback after establishing what the **named** target says they focus on.
- Governance and transparency still matter; weak or opaque structure can be a gap even for direct investors.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

1. **Attribution and relevance** matter for all investor types: whose track record is it, and is it in the right geography/stage/sector?
2. **Governance** is non-negotiable for DFIs and family offices; foundations and HNWIs care but may be slightly more flexible if thesis/relationship is strong.
3. **Impact is fund- and vehicle-specific.** Do not assume DFI = impact, family office = commercial, or HNWI = commercial. Many organisations have both impact and commercial vehicles. Only give impact feedback after searching for what the **named fund or vehicle** states they focus on.
4. **Terms and reporting** must align with investor type and expectations; mismatches are a common reason for “quiet no.”
5. **Verifiability** matters for family offices, HNWIs, and DFIs; foundations often rely more on relationship and thesis.
6. **First-time funds** can work for all four types if: track record (pre-fund) is clear and relevant, governance and impact (where the named fund requires it) are addressed, and the narrative is consistent and credible.

---

## Use in the skill

- When assessing a manager, consider **which investor type(s) and, if named, which specific fund(s)** they are targeting. Weight criteria accordingly (e.g. verifiability and terms for family office/HNWI; governance/ESG for DFI). **Do not** weight impact by org type alone; use the named fund's stated focus.
- **Impact feedback:** Only give feedback on impact track record **after** you have searched the web to find what the **fund or vehicle named by the user** says they focus on. Tailor impact comments to that; cite the source. If no named fund is given, do not raise impact as a criterion or gap.
- When defining “what track record needs to look like,” this document explains **why** Western LPs care about attribution, relevance, proof, and verifiability — and how emphasis differs by investor type and by fund/vehicle.
- Use this to refine track-record.md and SKILL.md so assessments are LP-type-aware and **fund-specific** where the user names target LPs or funds.
